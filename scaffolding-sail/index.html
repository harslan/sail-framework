Strategic Management · Suffolk University · Fall 2025

SAIL Framework Integration

S — Social Intelligence: Communicating AI use to stakeholders

A — AI Literacy: Understanding capabilities and limitations

I — Innovation/Inquiry: Questioning outputs, pushing beyond first answers

L — Leadership: Owning decisions and designing collaboration frameworks

The Challenge

AI presents a paradox for education. Students need to learn how to use these powerful tools, but unrestricted use can undermine the very learning we're trying to achieve. How do we prepare students for an AI-augmented workplace while ensuring they develop genuine expertise?

The answer isn't to ban AI or to embrace it uncritically. It's to scaffold — to progressively expand AI access as students build the capacity to use it wisely.

The Three-Phase Approach

This model, piloted in Strategic Management (Fall 2025), develops all four SAIL competencies through a structured progression:

1 Foundation: Restricted AI Use

What Students Do

Complete analytical work independently. AI is constrained to a "Research Assistant / Teaching Assistant" role through a custom system prompt — it can help find information and ask clarifying questions, but students must generate their own thesis, analysis, and conclusions.

Why It Matters

Students build baseline competence first. They develop the capacity to evaluate AI output because they've done the work themselves. Without this foundation, students can't distinguish good AI output from bad.

SAIL Focus: Social Intelligence + AI Literacy — Learning to communicate with AI appropriately and understanding its proper role.
2 Integration: Full AI with Critical Evaluation

What Students Do

Use AI without restrictions for assignments. However, they must actively evaluate outputs for hallucinations, logical errors, and relevance. They're required to reflect on how AI use affected their learning process.

Why It Matters

This phase develops metacognitive awareness — students learn to watch their own thinking. They experience firsthand how easy it is to accept AI output uncritically, and they develop strategies to resist that pull.

SAIL Focus: Innovation/Inquiry + Social Intelligence — Questioning outputs and developing the judgment to evaluate AI contributions.
3 Leadership: Designing Human-AI Collaboration

What Students Do

Take on the role of Chief Strategy Officer responsible for a team of 12 analysts. They must design a Human-AI collaboration framework that balances throughput, quality control, and knowledge building. The deliverable is a governance framework, not just personal AI use.

Why It Matters

Students shift from users to leaders. They're no longer just asking "How do I use AI?" but "How should my team use AI?" This develops the Leadership competency — taking responsibility for outcomes at scale.

SAIL Focus: Leadership — Owning decisions about Human-AI collaboration and designing systems that work for others.

The Collaboration Pattern

Human → AI → Human → AI

Effective collaboration is iterative, not one-directional. Humans set direction, AI contributes, humans evaluate and refine, AI executes.

What We Learned

The "Mesmerizing Force" of AI

Students consistently reported an almost hypnotic quality to AI interaction. The speed and fluency of responses created a pull toward acceptance rather than evaluation. Recognizing this psychological dimension — not just the technical capabilities — is essential for developing genuine AI literacy.

"One of the most significant obstacles that I encountered was to stay critical about myself when the AI made the process of writing so simple and quick. Since it generated entire paragraphs in real-time, it was too easy to receive the responses as final rather than evaluating whether they were relevant to our class structures or not. I observed that the more I was depending on it, the less I was thinking about the concepts of underlying strategies."

— Student reflection, Strategic Management, Fall 2025

This student captured precisely what researchers call cognitive debt — the accumulated cost of outsourcing thinking to AI. The three-phase scaffold helps students recognize this pattern and develop strategies to maintain their own intellectual agency.

The goal isn't to use AI less. It's to use AI wisely.

Adapting This Approach

The three-phase scaffold is designed to be flexible. The core principle — progressive expansion of AI access as students build capacity — can be adapted to any discipline:

Phase 1 might look different in accounting (manual calculations before spreadsheet AI), marketing (hand-drawn campaign concepts before generative tools), or finance (fundamental analysis before algorithmic assistance). The key is ensuring students build baseline competence first.

Phase 2 requires explicit reflection on AI use. This can be embedded in existing assignments or added as a metacognitive component.

Phase 3 works whenever students can take on a leadership perspective. Case competitions, capstone projects, and consulting simulations are natural fits.
