AI Has Judgment Now. So What's Left?

Why we updated our award-winning framework — and what it means for every business school.

By Hasan Arslan, PhD & Aykut Firat, PhD · SAIL Collaborative · Suffolk University

Last spring, one of our students said something that changed how we think about AI education.

Her team was mid-competition — our Prompt AIChemy challenge, 100,000 real e-commerce orders, AI generating strategic recommendations — when she paused. The AI had done its job well. It segmented customers, identified regional patterns, surfaced anomalies. It made good calls about what to prioritize. It exercised something that functioned like judgment.

But the AI missed something the students didn't.

Their recommendations would optimize seller performance metrics — but in doing so would penalize sellers in rural regions who lacked access to reliable logistics infrastructure. The data didn't flag this. The AI didn't flag it. The students did.

"We need to make sure we're not penalizing rural sellers with poor infrastructure."

— Unprompted

Nobody prompted them. No rubric required it. They raised it because they felt responsible for what their recommendations would do to actual people.

We've replayed that moment a hundred times. The AI had better judgment about the data. The students had something the AI structurally cannot have: they cared what happened next.

The Thesis We Had to Change

For two years, our SAIL Framework — the AI leadership model we built at Suffolk University, published in AACSB Insights, won an Eduventures Innovation Award for — was anchored in a thesis we believed deeply:

"Skills can be automated. Judgment cannot."

It was a powerful line. And it was honest — when we wrote it.

But AI moved. In the past few months, something shifted. Models stopped merely executing instructions and started making intelligent decisions — prioritizing, weighing tradeoffs, exercising what can only be called judgment. Anyone still claiming AI lacks judgment either hasn't used the current models or has an incentive to downplay what's happening.

If we tell students "judgment cannot be automated" and they watch AI demonstrate judgment in real time, we lose credibility. And credibility is the one thing a framework cannot survive without.

So the question became: if judgment is no longer the uniquely human thing, what is?

That student gave us the answer before we knew the question had changed.

The Word We Were Missing

The word is ownership.

AI can analyze a contract. AI can recommend a strategy. AI can flag a risk. AI can exercise judgment and taste. What AI cannot do is walk into a room and say: "This was my call. I weighed the tradeoffs. I considered who it would affect. And I'm accountable for the outcome."

Not because AI isn't smart enough. It may be smarter than any of us. But because ownership requires something no model possesses: skin in the game. A career that rises or falls on the decision. A reputation built on standing behind your work. A felt sense of responsibility for the people your decisions touch.

So we updated our thesis. Three words became nine:

Skills can be automated. Judgment can be augmented. Ownership cannot.

The Danger Nobody Is Talking About

This week, a viral essay on AI — over 20 million views — described a workflow where the author tells AI what he wants, walks away for four hours, and returns to find the work done. "Better than I would have done it myself," he writes. He predicts that nothing done on a screen is safe in the medium term.

He's probably right. But buried in his advice section is an insight more important than his headline: that roles requiring "licensed accountability — roles where someone still has to sign off, take legal responsibility, stand in a courtroom" will take longer to displace.

He describes ownership perfectly. He just doesn't name it.

And that's the real danger MIT researchers are documenting when they study "cognitive debt" — the accumulated deficit that builds when people accept AI outputs without evaluation. The deeper problem isn't that people lose the ability to evaluate AI. It's that they lose the felt sense that the decisions are theirs. When you've outsourced enough thinking, you stop feeling like the owner. You become a relay station — passing AI outputs upstream without ever asking: would I stake my name on this?

Our students stepped into ownership. Unprompted.

That's the difference between using AI and leading with AI.

Why This Matters Now

Fear without a framework is paralysis. Adaptation without direction is drift.

What's missing from every "AI is coming for your job" essay is a clear answer to the question: If AI can do the cognitive work, what exactly should humans get better at?

The answer isn't judgment. AI has that now, or close enough.

The answer isn't creativity. AI is getting there.

The answer isn't empathy. People are already turning to AI for emotional support.

The answer is the willingness to own consequences in a world where it's increasingly easy not to.

"I used to think AI was about getting the right technical answer. Now I realize it's more like conducting an orchestra — you need to know when to bring in different instruments, but you also need to make sure the whole performance serves the audience."

— Suffolk Business Student

Conductors don't play every instrument. But they put their name on the program.

Every business school is scrambling to add AI to their curriculum. Very few are asking the harder question: what are we developing in students that AI cannot replicate? Not what AI can't do yet — what it cannot do structurally, by virtue of what it is?

We think the answer is ownership. And we think the schools that build their programs around developing it — not just teaching AI tools, but cultivating the courage to decide and the integrity to be accountable — will produce the graduates that organizations actually need.

Not AI-literate graduates. Not AI-savvy graduates.

Graduates who own what happens next.
