§ 01 — The Problem

AI is a good-story engine.
Valuation is a good-stock test.

In FIN 311 you'll value real companies using a 2-stage Dividend Discount Model and a relative P/E approach. Then you'll do something an AI cannot: weight the two estimates against each other and stake a recommendation on the result. That weighting — and your defense of it — is where analyst judgment lives. It is also exactly the kind of decision AI tools sound most confident about and get most often wrong.

The problem isn't that AI lies. The problem is that AI's lies are fluent. A fabricated analyst quote reads like a sourced one. A made-up "industry standard weighting" sounds like guidance. The gap between borrowed confidence and earned judgment is invisible — until someone asks you to defend it.

"To be a good analyst, you must be able to differentiate between a good story and a good stock." Dr. Nataliya Zaiats — FIN 311 Project Description

That line was written before generative AI made fluent storytelling free. The skill it names — separating narrative quality from valuation quality — has not gotten easier in 2026. It has gotten harder. And more valuable.

§ 02 — Two Analysts

Two students. One Colab.
Only one of them gets the offer.

Both run the same Valuation Analytics notebook. Both produce a price target. The difference shows up the moment someone asks why.

The student who owns the analysis

01
Writes a one-paragraph thesis first. Guesses beta and growth before any cell runs.Now the Colab outputs are checks, not answers.
02
Notices recent growth diverges from the 5-year average. Documents which one to use and why.Choice becomes visible. Defensible.
03
Weights DDM vs P/E based on the company's specifics — payout history, sector dispersion, earnings volatility."60/40 because the firm has 15 years of stable dividends and the sector P/E has 30% dispersion."
04
In the defense round: explains every assumption, names what would change the answer.The Reframe scenario lands. Recommendation updates with reasoning.
The judgment is theirs. The defense holds.

§ 04 — The Challenge

The Valuation AIChemist Challenge

The two-class in-lab redesign of FIN 311's Valuation Analytics Project. The Colab notebook is the engine. SAIL is the steering. Four phases, one keystone moment, one defense that cannot be borrowed.

i
Phase 1 · Class 1

Think First

Before any Colab cell runs: one paragraph thesis on the company. Guess beta. Guess 5-year dividend growth. Guess intrinsic value. Pen and paper. No notebook open.

25 min · No AI · No code
ii
Phase 2 · Class 1

AI Augment

Run the Colab. At every STUDENT decision point — beta method, growth rate choice, horizon year — write a one-line justification in the Decision Log next to the cell. Compare to your Phase 1 guess.

50 min · Colab + Decision Log
iii
Phase 3 · Class 2

Reframe

Each team draws one of three scenario cards. Eight minutes to revise the recommendation. Twelve minutes to write down what changed in the weighting and why. This is the test of ownership.

20 min · Scenario disruption
iv
Phase 4 · Class 2

Defense

Three-minute pair defense. Partner asks: "Why those weights? What would change your answer? Where exactly did this number come from?" If you can't defend it, it isn't yours.

40 min · Live Q&A
▲ The Keystone Moment

Your DDM says one price. Your P/E says another. How do you weight them?

This is the question the syllabus already asks: "[Y]our final estimate of the stock price should be the weighted average of the stock prices obtained from DDM and valuation using multiples. You must justify your choice of weights." Everything in the Challenge is designed to make this moment defensible.

Which weighting is correct?

None of them is automatically correct. All four answers can be right or wrong depending on the company. "It depends" is closer to the truth than any number — but only if you can name what it depends on.

A defensible weighting requires you to identify, in writing, at least two company-specific reasons: dividend stability favors DDM weight; tight comparable firm density favors P/E weight; high earnings volatility undermines P/E; uncertain growth horizon undermines DDM. The percentages are downstream. The reasoning is the work.

Artifact 01 · Print three cards · One per team

Reframe Scenario Cards

Drawn at the start of Phase 3. Each card disrupts a specific input to the valuation — dividend, sector multiple, beta — and forces the team to revisit the weighting they committed to in Phase 2. Eight minutes to revise, two minutes to defend, one line to commit.

Card 01
The Dividend Cut

Capital reallocation announcement

Overnight, your company announces a 40% dividend cut, citing "capital reallocation toward growth investments." Stock price reacts −7% pre-market. Fundamentals unchanged.
  • What does this do to your DDM estimate? Walk through which inputs move.
  • Does it change your P/E estimate? Why or why not?
  • You weighted DDM at 60%. Re-weight or hold? Defend in two sentences.
  • New recommendation. One line. Sign it.
Tests · DDM sensitivity 8 min
Card 02
The Sector Re-Rating

A Fed move resets the multiple

A surprise FOMC statement causes your sector's average P/E to compress from 22× to 14× in three days. Your company's fundamentals are unchanged. Dividend policy is unchanged.
  • What does this do to your P/E valuation? Show the new number.
  • Does it change your DDM estimate? Why or why not?
  • Has the company become a better buy, worse buy, or no change?
  • This is the good story / good stock test. Which one moved?
Tests · Multiples vs DDM 8 min
Card 03
The Beta Spike

Acquisition integration risk

Your company announces a $1.2B acquisition of a smaller competitor. Analysts re-estimate beta from 0.85 to 1.40 reflecting integration and leverage risk. Earnings guidance is unchanged.

  • What does this do to your DDM estimate?
  • Does P/E valuation move? How?

Artifact 02 · Replaces existing CG article write-up format

Redesigned CG Article Write-Up

The current half-to-one-page summary plus five takeaway bullets is precisely the format ChatGPT produces best. Below is a redesigned format that makes AI summarization actively unhelpful: it moves the assignment from summary recall (where AI is strong) to application judgment (where AI is weak). Same length. Same grading time. Genuinely different cognitive demand.

The four-part response · 1 page single-spaced

  1. Argument summary · 4–6 sentences. Not a recap of the paper's structure. State what the author is trying to prove and the central mechanism by which they prove it. If you cannot do this in five sentences, you have not understood the paper.
  2. Current-event application · 1 paragraph. Identify one specific publicly traded firm where this paper's argument applies to a corporate event in the last 60 days. Name the company, name the event, explain the connection. "Many companies face governance issues" is not an answer.
  3. The weak claim · 1 paragraph. Identify one specific claim, assumption, or empirical choice in the paper that you find weakest, and explain why a corporate board reading this paper should be skeptical of it. Disagreement is required, not optional.
  4. The unanswered question · 2 sentences. Identify one question a board considering implementing the paper's recommendation would need answered that the paper does not address. This is the original-thinking test.
Why this design. ChatGPT can produce part 1 trivially. It can fake part 2. It will produce a generic part 3 that lacks specificity to the actual empirical choices in the paper. It cannot reliably produce part 4 without the student doing real work. Grade weight should shift accordingly: 15% / 30% / 30% / 25%. The summary becomes a comprehension check, not the assignment.
Artifact 03 · One-page student deliverable · Part of lab submission

Transparency Appendix Template

Replaces the existing peer/self evaluation form for the lab. Submitted by each student individually. Half a page if the student used AI minimally; one page if they used it heavily. The audit trail is the deliverable. No AI policy violation; full documentation is the policy.

Transparency Appendix · One page · Submit individually

  1. Phase 1 written thesis — attach or paste your one-paragraph pre-Colab thesis. Three numbers: your guessed beta, your guessed 5-year dividend growth, your guessed intrinsic value.
  2. Decision Log — for each STUDENT decision point in the Colab (beta method, growth rate choice, horizon year, weighting), one sentence of justification. Five entries minimum.
  3. AI usage log — list every AI interaction during the lab (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, etc.). For each: what you asked, what it gave you, what you kept, what you changed, what you rejected. "None" is a valid entry if true.
  4. Verification notes — for each piece of AI-supplied information you used, what primary source confirmed it (10-K page, earnings transcript, Bloomberg, FRED, Yahoo Finance). If unverified, mark explicitly.
  5. What changed between Phase 1 and Phase 4 — one sentence. Did your initial thesis hold? What forced a revision? What did you learn that you could not have learned by running the notebook alone?
Grading. Honesty is graded, not minimal use. A student who used AI heavily and documented it carefully should score higher than one who used AI casually and reported "none." The skill is process accountability — the same standard CFA Standard V(C) Record Retention requires of professional analysts.
Artifact 04 · Replaces existing line in project description

Syllabus AI Policy Revision

The current FIN 311 project description contains the line "AI-generated reports are unacceptable." That language was written for the take-home team project. The in-class lab implicitly invites AI engagement, so the policy needs an update that works for both deliverables. The replacement is one paragraph; it doesn't ban AI and it doesn't endorse it — it requires what professional standards already require.

Replacement language · Project Description, p. 2

AI-generated reports are unacceptable.

Use of generative AI tools in this course is permitted and, in the Valuation Analytics Lab, expected. What is required is process transparency. Every student must submit a Transparency Appendix documenting which AI tools were used, what was asked, what was kept, changed, or rejected, and which primary sources were used to verify any AI-supplied information. AI-generated reports submitted as the student's own work — without documentation, verification, or evidence of judgment — will be treated as academic dishonesty under the same standard as fabricated sources or plagiarized text. Cite AI tools the same way you cite a Bloomberg terminal: with the date, the prompt, and the role it played in your work.
